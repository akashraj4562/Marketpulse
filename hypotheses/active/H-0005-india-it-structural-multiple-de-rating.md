## Statement
**Cause:** India IT's revenue growth is decoupling from employment growth for the first time structurally — FY26 saw $315B revenue but only 140,000 net new jobs vs 600,000 in FY22. This signals that AI tooling has fundamentally changed the revenue-per-employee ratio. As this becomes visible in earnings data (slower revenue growth, lower headcount, billing rate pressure), the historically premium PE multiple (22–25x) that Indian IT commanded — justified by high-growth, high-certainty earnings compounding — is no longer sustainable.

**Effect:** India IT sector PE multiple re-rates from ~22–25x (historical) to ~14–18x over 12–18 months. This implies 20–35% additional downside on a PE compression basis alone (even if earnings grow modestly). Total return for large-cap IT stocks: flat to -20% over 18 months, while Nifty 50 grows 10–15%.

**One-line:** AI revenue-employment decoupling → India IT high-growth earnings certainty story breaks → PE de-rates from 22–25x to 14–18x, over 12–18 months.

**Capital market thesis:**
India IT's PE premium was predicated on two factors that are now eroding: (a) high and predictable earnings growth (15–20% CAGR historically), and (b) a large addressable market with long runway. AI tools are compressing (a) faster than the market has priced, particularly for the annuity-type contracts (maintenance, support, BPO) that form the stable base of revenue. The historical analog is instructive: when US telecom lost its growth narrative post-2000, the sector re-rated from 30x+ PE to 10–15x over 3 years. India IT won't fall that far (it has genuine global competitive advantage in talent and scale) but a transition from 22–25x to 14–18x is the base case. The specific mechanism: FY27 Q1 earnings (reported July 2026) will be the first quarter where the billing volume slowdown becomes visible in revenue growth numbers. Watch TCS and Infosys revenue guidance for Q1 FY27 — that is the thesis confirmation event.
